Diana Huang

Mechmania 2018

Gameplay, UI, and tools programmer of 24 hour AI hackathon | August 2017 - September 2018

Mechmania is a 24 hour AI hackathon held at the annual Reflections Projections conference at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign.

I am a developer of the hackathon, specifically a member of the visualizer team. My teammates and I were responsible for receiving streams of game data and visualizing the data in Unity3D. I implemented a custom JSON parser to bridge the gap between the game engine team and the visualizer team, created a map generator for play testing, implemented the dynamic camera movement, designed the final map, and implemented some UI elements.

Team Work

The team consisted on 10 undergraduate students at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign. We were divided into the visualizer team, game engine team (whose job was to run the game logic and output game logs), and the infrastructure team.

One of the challenges we faced was the presence of dependencies in our complex workflow. For instance, I could not finalize my JSON parser if the game engine team did not finalize the structure of their game logs. And members of the visualizer team could not begin their work until the JSON parser was finished. I clearly communicated task dependancies which prevented my teammembers from becoming blocked. Overall, we worked cohesively as a group and proudly presented a great MechMania tournament. I am excited to work with the team again for MechMania 2019.

Technical Work

The code I wrote for the visualizer challenged my ability to break down large tasks into bite-sized, easy to test modular units. For example, one of my tasks was to write a map generator that takes in graph data (coordinates of nodes, list of edges) and visualizes it within Unity. I split up my tasks into instantiating the nodes at the given positions, calculating the Quaternion rotations of the respective edges with a bit of linear algebra, resolving the lengths of the edges depending on how far away its respective nodes were, and refining the map so there is no unnatural looking merging of gameobjects. Doing so made debugging my code much easier and increased the readability of my code.

Working on the visualizer team requires a unique combination between technical ability and artistic instinct. Because there were no artists on the team, collaborating with the team members to figure out the final look of the game was important. I worked primarily on the final map design, which I made sure to polish with non-repetitive tiles and particle effects to make the world feel more alive.

Statistics

MechMania 2018 had many firsts with the use of a new client system, a language agnostic option for contestants, and live twitch streams of the matches.

While Mechmania was streaming, it was the top programming stream on twitch for its duration with a total of 585 viewers.

A total of 41 teams entered with 37 teams finishing the competition.

1382 bots were submitted and 43,011 total matches were played. All resulting in a total number of ~9558 computed hours.

More information about the tournament and its history can be found here: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MechMania

My Experience

Developing MechMania was one of my greatest experiences at this university. It taught me many invaluable lessons about how to work effectively and efficiently in a team, which is a lesson rarely learned in the school curriculum. I was fortunate to work with some of the brightest students I've met at UIUC and make plenty of friends along the way. The nights of debugging and stressful game design could be considered grueling, but it all payed off when we watched our game on the big screen and heard the competitors devising intense strategies to win. For many of us, it was our first shipped game and it feels fantastic when you realize that you've just taken the first step into your game development career because of it. I will be part of the MechMania 2019 staff so check in in a year for updates!
